# Regional cluster economics

The financial logic behind clustering clinics geographically. Pawline's operating thesis is that 8-15 clinics within a 90-minute drive of one shared flagship is the unit of profitability.

## Why clusters

- **One surgical suite, many GP clinics**: a Tier A surgical suite serves 6-12 surrounding Tier B/C clinics. Below 6, the suite under-utilizes; above 12, it backs up.
- **Lab routing**: a single lab pickup route serving 8-15 clinics on the same Antech contract is materially cheaper than per-clinic relationships
- **Shared specialist**: a cardiologist or internist 2-5 days/week across 6-10 clinics is economically viable; one-clinic specialty is not
- **RMD/ROD coverage**: a dyad pair can meaningfully oversee 8-15 clinics; beyond 15, attention spreads thin

## Density math

[[regional-density-clinics-correlate-with-margin]]: regions with >12 clinics show 4-6 ppt margin uplift over regions with <8. The break-even is around 8-9 clinics. Below that, fixed regional overhead (RMD, ROD, area ops manager, lab logistics) eats the margin.

## Why this drives M&A geography

Pawline's M&A scoring model in [[acquisition-criteria]] penalizes targets that don't fit an existing cluster or that don't enable a new cluster. A great clinic in a poor location is a worse acquisition than a good clinic in a great location. [[texas-acquisition-pipeline-thinning]] is precisely this problem — TX targets don't cluster well at our preferred size.

## Tradeoffs

- Concentration risk: regional shocks (regulatory, weather, economic) hit harder
- Talent pool concentration: doctor recruiting in a tight cluster competes with itself
- Brand uniformity: tightly clustered Pawline clinics are visibly Pawline; some markets prefer that, some don't
