# Urgent care vs ER vs GP

Pawline's three service-line definitions, each with distinct staffing, equipment, and operating economics.

## Definitions

- **General Practice (GP)** — wellness, routine sick visits, dental, basic surgery (spays/neuters), preventive care. Scheduled appointments. ~85% of Pawline volume.
- **Urgent Care (UC)** — same-day, walk-in, non-life-threatening cases. Typical examples: ear infections after-hours, lacerations, mild GI issues. Currently offered as an extended-hours wing of select Tier A and Tier B clinics; ~10% of Pawline volume.
- **ER / Specialty** — life-threatening or specialist-required care: trauma, blocked cats, dystocia, oncology consultations, cardiology workups. ~5% of Pawline volume, concentrated in Tier A flagships with 24-hour staffing.

## Why the distinction matters

Each service line has fundamentally different unit economics:

- GP: scheduled flow, mid-margin, doctor-bound capacity
- UC: walk-in flow, premium-priced visits, technician-leveraged
- ER: high acuity, premium pricing, expensive equipment + 24-hour staffing burden

Mixing them poorly is a known failure mode in vet rollups. Banfield's experience (cited often by [[dr-amelia-foster]]) is that running ER inside a GP clinic compromises both: GP scheduling gets disrupted by walk-ins, and ER quality suffers from generalist staff.

## When to refer

- GP → UC: same day if scheduled appointment unavailable but case warrants today
- GP/UC → ER: triage red ([[triage-acuity-levels]]), or anything requiring 24-hour observation
- ER → external specialty: tertiary care (e.g., neurosurgery, advanced oncology) where Pawline doesn't have specialty capability

## The open strategic question

[[2026-03-30-emergency-clinic-line-of-service]] is whether to acquire a freestanding ER/specialty network or build organically inside flagships. The answer hinges on whether the ER line can stand on its own economics.
